Coping with anxiety: A strategy

Requirements

One large piece of paper. A writing implement.

Foreword

We will need to practise this strategy to integrate it into our lives. Once it’s integrated, parts of it will become second nature.

This strategy is not a quick fix; it should take a little while to understand. It aims to give a sense of constructive control and autonomy over the things we can change.

As we try to write down our feelings and stresses, we may feel emotional. Crying is allowed.

Sometimes it may be difficult to write out our concerns. Other people may read our notes or we might not want to write something down. That is fine.

We are going to utilise an analysis technique that creates two extremes; internal/external, emotional/physical, thoughts/environment, good/bad. There are grey areas between each of those two extremes. We should not worry.

The activity of writing down our feelings helps us to reflect on them.

This is a plan for how to write down our feelings and how to think about them.

LEARNING: Basic needs

Humans are social animals who live in a society. Life is complicated. Here we consider our basic animal needs.

  1. Food
  2. Water
  3. Sleep
  4. Exercise
  5. Nature
  6. Security
  7. Privacy
  8. Company
  9. Feeling effective and useful (agency)

Developing effective coping mechanisms usually involve changing our coping behaviours to satisfy these needs.

METHOD: Notice, reflect, retrain

When we notice that we're experiencing a negative situation, the first useful questions to ask ourselves are.

  • “Am I thirsty?”
  • “Am I tired?”
  • “Have I exercised?”
  • “Am I hungry?”
  • “Have I been outside?”
  • “Do I feel safe?”
  • “Do I need to be alone?”
  • “Am I lonely?”
  • “Can I do something rewarding?”

We relect on our basic needs.

Once we identify an issue, we want to train ourselves to develop small, repeatable, easy behaviours, in any small capacity, to help us cope more effectively.

  • “Get a glass of water”
  • “Have a nap”
  • “Do some stretches”
  • “Take some time for myself”
  • “Have a meal”
  • “Ring a friend”
  • “Talk to someone about my feelings”
  • “Make a date”

ACTION: Bad coping mechanisms

We now need to look at which of the bad coping mechanisms are factors in cause the negative physical, emotional or environmental issues and thoughts to become worse.

Link bad coping mechanisms to negative phenomenon, where they are related, with lines and arrows.

Think about how the bad coping mechanisms and negative experiences are related. Make notes on your thinking.

  • “I don’t sleep enough which makes me feel confused and tired all day”
  • “I’m constantly dehydrated”
  • “Smoking makes me constantly anxious”
  • “I am abusive when I drink”

Write some notes on good, easy, quick behaviours that you can do which will improve your health and help you feel calmer.

ACTION: Future plans

Some of the negative phenomenon require longer term plans.

Identify from your negative thoughts what it is that you could do to feel better in the future.

These goals should be achievable and simple.

  • “Visit people more often”
  • “Eat one good meal a day”
  • “Tidy once every two weeks”
  • “Change jobs”
  • “Move house”
  • “Talk to someone about their behaviour”
  • “Stop smoking”

We can periodically take steps to achieve our goals. For example, finding a new job has many small steps, it is possible to spread those out and try to do one small step each week.

Lots of repeated effort goes a long way.

Talking to people about our issues will help give them perspective.

TOOLS: Linguistics

Language is very imprecise and limited.

Often people will explain their emotions in the words and actions they choose to use when they communicate.

Understanding how we communicate can give us insights into our thoughts and feelings.

The overwhelming majority of how we communicate is via our body language; body language evolved first, language evolved later. Most animals communicate with only their body language. The remaining part of our communication is through spoken and written language.

Consider these examples:

“No-one is calling or texting me”

The person using these words may feel lonely. They may attribute their sense of loneliness to other people’s deliberate lack of care. It is likely that the people in question are not intentionally non-communicative.

“Humans got herpes from fucking animals, didn’t they?”

This person is vocalising an obvious yet unspoken truth and seeking confirmation of the implied conclusions. The person sees other humans as sexually disgusting, this suggests some sexual trauma. The person probably has herpes and feels bad about it. There is a sense of injustice. The stated fact cannot be proven and does not support the implied conclusions.

How this helps:

We can listen to what we say and do, we can think about the emotions we are expressing and we can use that to help us discover our own repetitive negative emotions, thoughts and behaviours. With practise, we can listen better to others.
